## Figure reproduction comparison

The comparison graphs show how closely our reproduced Yang baseline matches the reported values from the original paper. We compare three outputs: the evolved strategy `c = (p(F|L), p(L|F), p(L|B))`, the positional frequencies `f = (f_L, f_F, f_B, f_C)`, and collective mobility (`CM`). Collective mobility is calculated as the proportion of prey occupying mobile-group positions, so in our normalized implementation `CM = f_L + f_F`. A high CM therefore means the population is mostly in leader/follower mobile groups, while a CM close to zero means the population is mostly in border/centre cohesive herds.

The reproduced values are close for Figure 1A and qualitatively correct for Figure 1B, but the Figure 2 cases, especially Figure 2B, differ more from the paper. This is expected because the original Yang experiments used a much larger computational setup: 1,000 prey agents, 40,000 interaction decisions per evaluation, 10% population replacement per generation, mutation with standard deviation 0.02, and 3,000 generations. Our reproduction used a smaller practical run to keep runtime manageable, so the GA has less time and less sampling resolution to settle. The difference is also scientifically meaningful rather than only a coding issue: Yang explicitly notes that cases where the centre is riskier than the border (`X_C > X_B`) are less converged, with strategy variation still remaining high even after 3,000 generations. For those cases, Yang estimates the final values using late-generation averages. Because Figure 2A and Figure 2B fall into this harder convergence regime, our shorter stochastic run can reproduce the overall pattern but not always the exact numerical strategy and frequency values.
